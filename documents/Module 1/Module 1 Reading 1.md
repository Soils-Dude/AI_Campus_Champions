**_Q1: How do Bommasani et al. define a 'foundation model'? What two properties (training approach + adaptation range) are central to the definition?_**

"A foundation model is any model that is trained on broad data (generally using self-supervision at scale) that can be adapted (e.g., fine-tuned) to a wide range of downstream tasks"

Training approach: Self-supervised

Adaption range: widely adopted

**_Q2: What does the paper mean by 'emergence,' and why does this property make foundation models difficult to predict even for their creators?_**

Emergence: The system is not specifically told to do a specific behavior but it can be done by the system anyway. Given that emergent properties are not designed in the models we can not predict exactly when they will appear, how they will behave, etc.

**_Q3: The authors identify 'homogenization' as a double-edged property. What are the opportunity and the risk they associate with it?_**

You can accomplish many tasks with one system but it will create a single point of failure at the same time and reduce the creation of novel ideas.

**_Key terms -- write your own definition after reading:_**

**_Term Your definition (in your own words)_**

**_Foundational model_** _-_ Any model that can be fine tuned for varying tasks that has been trained on broad data sets.

**_Fine-tuning -_** Process of adapting foundation models for specific tasks

**_In-context learning -_** Adapting a foundation model for a specific task via a prompt

**_Emergence -_** The system is not specifically told to do a specific behavior but it can be done by the system anyway.

**_Homogeneization -_** The process by which a single foundation model will function for a series of unrelated or loosely related tasks.
