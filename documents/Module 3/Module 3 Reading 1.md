# Module 3 · Reading 1 - Guided Reading Question Responses

## Guided Reading Questions

**Q1. What type of tasks were used in the Noy & Zhang experiment? Are these tasks representative of the most cognitively demanding work in your professional role?**

The experiment used short (20-30 minute), occupation-specific, incentivized writing tasks: press releases, short reports, analysis plans, and sensitive ("delicate") emails. Participants came from six white-collar occupations, marketers, grant writers, consultants, data analysts, HR professionals, and managers, and rated the tasks as realistic versions of their everyday work. Each output was graded 1-7 by blinded professional peers. Crucially, the tasks were self-contained and, as the authors note, "lack a dimension of context-specific knowledge."

**In my context:** these map onto the routine writing-and-communication slice of a lab manager's week - drafting emails, internal memos, results summaries, first-draft SOPs, ordering justifications, and narrative sections of progress reports. They are not representative of my most cognitively demanding work: experimental and sampling design, troubleshooting and calibrating instruments, QA/QC and data-interpretation judgment calls, method development, safety risk assessment, and training student staff. Those depend on tacit, context-specific knowledge of our samples, instruments, and protocols, exactly the dimension the study removes, so the findings speak to roughly a fifth of my work, not its core.

**Q2. The study found that AI "compressed" the skill distribution - lower-skilled workers improved more. What are the institutional implications of this finding for how AI tools are deployed in higher-education staff contexts?**

In the treatment group the correlation between first- and second-task grades fell from 0.49 to 0.25 - initial inequality was roughly halved. Lower-scoring workers gained the most (higher quality and less time), while high scorers mainly held their quality and got faster. AI raised the floor more than the ceiling.

**Implications for a university:** (1) AI can act as a leveler, helping newer staff, student workers, and non-native English writers produce more consistent routine documents, an equity-and-capacity argument for broad access. (2) But if a tool flattens the visible quality gap between novice and expert writing, written deliverables become a weaker signal of who actually holds the underlying skill, so evaluation and training must look past polished output to the judgment beneath it. (3) Noy & Zhang found the gains came mostly from substituting for effort, not building skill - so "leveling up" staff output can happen without leveling up competence. In a lab that is a real hazard: an AI-polished method summary from someone who does not understand the method is more dangerous than an awkward one from someone who does. Deployment should therefore pair access with training, disclosure norms, and a verification step, and reserve the leveling benefit for low-stakes, low-sensitivity writing rather than interpretation or anything touching protected data.

One caveat: the compression is measured by overall baseline performance. The authors separately found no clear evidence that people with weak writing skills relative to their other skills benefited more, so "AI helps the weakest writers specifically" is not cleanly supported by this study.

**Q3. The paper found quality improvements in addition to time savings. What metric was used to assess "quality," and is that metric applicable to the documents you produce?**

Quality was human-judged: experienced professionals in the same occupations, blinded to whether AI was used, scored each piece on a 1-7 scale, an overall grade plus separate grades for writing quality, content quality, and originality. Three evaluators graded each output, and their average agreement was only moderate (cross-evaluator correlation 0.44).

**In my context:** only partly. For subjective communication products, outreach, newsletters, narrative report sections, emails; "would an experienced peer rate this as well written" transfers reasonably well. But the dominant quality criterion for most lab documents is factual and numerical correctness and protocol fidelity: right molarity, consistent units, an SOP that matches what the instrument actually requires, correctly transcribed data, accurate safety information. A subjective 1-7 polish score does not capture that; a document can read as a 7 and still be wrong in a way that ruins a run or a dataset. The modest 0.44 inter-rater agreement also shows that even trained humans disagree about "quality." So, the study's metric maps onto the readability of my writing, not the correctness that matters most in the lab.

**Q4. What is one methodological limitation of this study that would affect your confidence in applying its findings to your specific work context?**

The most important one, which the authors themselves flag: the tasks were short, self-contained, and deliberately stripped of context-specific knowledge, which "may inflate our estimates of ChatGPT's usefulness." Their own two-week follow-up showed that non-users mostly stayed away because their real writing needs context the model lacks. Because nearly all of my work is context-specific, our samples, instruments, calibrations, and data, the headline 37% time saving and ~0.4-SD quality gain are best read as an upper bound that overstates what I would realistically get on real lab documents.

**Other limitations worth noting:** 10-20% of the control group used ChatGPT anyway (making the headline effects a lower bound in the other direction), the study tested a single tool and model (free ChatGPT, GPT-3.5, late 2022), it covered only writing, not data analysis or lab work, and it measured immediate effects only, with no learning-curve or longer-term dynamics.

## Key Terms (my definitions)

| **Term**                              | **My definition (in my own words)**                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 |
| ------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Randomized controlled trial (RCT)** | A study that randomly assigns participants to a treatment group (here, given ChatGPT) or a control group (not), so the groups differ on average only by the treatment, letting differences in outcome be attributed to the tool rather than to who chose to use it. |
| **Treatment effect**                  | The causal difference in an outcome produced by the treatment, here, about a 37% reduction in time and a ~0.45-SD rise in graded quality from ChatGPT access, relative to control.                                                                                  |
| **Skill compression**                 | When a tool helps lower-performing workers more than higher-performing ones, shrinking the gap between them. Here the first-task-to-second-task grade correlation fell from 0.49 to 0.25.                                                                           |
| **Task completion time**              | How long it takes to finish a defined task from start to finish; the study's main productivity measure (control ≈27 min vs. treated ≈17 min on the second task).                                                                                                    |
| **Output quality assessment**         | The procedure for judging how good the produced work is, here, blinded peers scoring each piece 1-7 on overall, writing, content, and originality, with three graders per piece.                                                                                    |
