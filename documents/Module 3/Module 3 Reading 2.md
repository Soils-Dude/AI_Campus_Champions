# Module 3 · Reading 2 - Guided Reading Question Responses

## Guided Reading Questions

**Q1. What is the "jagged frontier" and why is it "jagged" rather than a smooth capability boundary?**

The jagged frontier is the uneven, expanding boundary of what current AI can do well. Inside it, AI matches or beats humans and lifts both speed and quality; just outside it, AI output is inaccurate and can actively degrade human performance. It is "jagged" rather than smooth because the boundary is irregular and unpredictable: tasks that look equally difficult to a person can fall on opposite sides some that seem hard are easy for AI (idea generation, drafting), while some that seem easy are hard for it (e.g., certain basic math, or reconciling conflicting data). The shape is not obvious even to the models' own developers, and a single workflow routinely contains subtasks on both sides. A smooth frontier would mean AI difficulty tracks human-perceived difficulty; the paper's point is that it does not.

**Q2. How did Dell'Acqua et al. identify which tasks were "inside" vs. "outside" the frontier experimentally? What implications does this classification method have for practitioners who cannot run experiments?**

They identified the sides by design and testing, not by intuition. They deliberately built two tasks - one engineered to sit inside the frontier and one engineered to sit outside and validated them. The inside task was actually a set of 18 realistic consulting tasks (creative ideation, analysis, persuasive writing) that GPT-4 handles well. The outside task was a business problem-solving case constructed as a subtle trap: the quantitative data in a spreadsheet pointed to a tempting but wrong answer, while the correct answer required reconciling that data with qualitative interview notes and the model tended to seize the misleading quantitative pattern and produce a confident, wrong recommendation. Comparing AI versus no-AI performance then confirmed each task's side (inside: AI better; outside: AI users 19 percentage points less likely to be correct).

**In my context:** practitioners usually cannot pre-classify tasks this way, the experiment's whole point is that the boundary is non-obvious and only became clear through testing. So classification has to be treated as an ongoing, empirical, trial-and-error problem: probe a task, check the output against ground truth, compare notes with colleagues, and never assume that "simple-looking" means "safe for AI." For my lab that means building a running map of what is reliably inside (formatting a methods section, drafting an email, summarizing a document) versus outside (anything depending on our specific numbers, calibration logic, or interpretive judgment), and verifying before trusting.

**Q3. What does the "centaur" collaboration style mean in this paper? What does the "cyborg" style mean? Which is more appropriate for the task you will complete in Task 2?**

Centaur style is a clear division of labor: you decide which subtasks suit the human and which suit the AI and hand whole chunks to each based on their strengths, switching at the task boundary (the term comes from Kasparov's "centaur" chess). Cyborg style is deep integration: you intertwine with the AI at the subtask level in continuous back-and-forth starting a sentence for it to finish, having it validate a line, immediately re-prompting so the human/AI boundary is blurred throughout.

**For my Task 2:** a low-stakes lab writing task such as a routine internal email, a first-draft SOP, or a results summary is best done centaur-style. Let the AI take the frontier-inside chunk (structure, first-draft prose, formatting) while I keep the frontier-outside chunks the technical facts, numbers, safety details, interpretation, and final sign-off firmly in human hands. The clean split also makes the required verification step and the disclosure decision easier. A cyborg style is fine for the pure-prose portions, but it gets risky the moment the task touches data or protocol, because tight integration makes it easy to absorb a wrong suggestion without noticing, the over-reliance failure the paper warns about. (Realistically I may be a cyborg within the writing subtask but a centaur at the level of the whole task; the authors note people mix both.)

**Q4. The paper found performance degradation for AI-assisted workers on outside-frontier tasks. What cognitive mechanism do the authors propose to explain this? Do you find the explanation convincing?**

The proposed mechanism is over-reliance what the lead author elsewhere calls "falling asleep at the wheel," and what this paper describes as "unengaged interaction with AI." On outside-frontier tasks the AI produces fluent, confident, professional-looking output that happens to be wrong, and the consultants who did worst tended to adopt it blindly and interrogate it less, switching off their own judgment. The effect is amplified because AI raised the apparent polish of recommendations even when the underlying answer was wrong, so the persuasive packaging makes the error harder to catch; the authors track this with a "retainment" / blind-adoption measure and stress that the fix is to keep validating and interrogating the AI.

**Is it convincing?** largely yes, it is backed by a behavioral measure, by the "wrong-but-polished" quality result, and it converges with older research on automation complacency and with everyday experience of confident-but-wrong AI output. The honest caveat is that "over-reliance" partly relabels the outcome (people who deferred more did worse), so it risks circularity unless the blind-adoption measure is truly independent of performance, and the outside-frontier evidence rests on essentially one engineered trap task. I would want several more outside-frontier tasks before treating the mechanism as fully general but as a working explanation it is plausible and well-aligned with the data.

## Key Terms (my definitions)

| **Term**                          | **My definition (in my own words)**                                                                                                                                                                  |
| --------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Jagged technological frontier** | The uneven, shifting boundary of tasks AI does well; inside it AI helps, just outside it AI hurts, and the edge is irregular and hard to predict.                                                    |
| **Centaur collaboration**         | Human-AI teamwork by clear division of labor assigning whole subtasks to whichever party (human or AI) is better and switching at task boundaries.                                                   |
| **Cyborg collaboration**          | Human-AI teamwork by tight integration - interleaving human and AI work at the subtask level in continuous back-and-forth.                                                                           |
| **Over-reliance**                 | Uncritically accepting AI output ("falling asleep at the wheel") deferring to it and reducing your own scrutiny, which causes errors, especially on outside-frontier tasks.                          |
| **Inside-frontier task**          | A task within current AI capability, where AI use raises speed and quality (e.g., the 18 consulting tasks, ideation, drafting, analysis).                                                            |
| **Outside-frontier task**         | A task beyond reliable AI capability, where AI output is inaccurate and tends to degrade performance (e.g., the trap problem-solving case, where AI users were 19 points less likely to be correct). |
